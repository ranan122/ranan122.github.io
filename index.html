About

Studying how children grow and what that tells us about intelligence

My research begins with a deceptively simple question: how do infants learn to move, communicate, and connect with the people around them? During my graduate career at the University of Pittsburgh, I have been exploring the emergence of motor and language skills, and how parent-child interaction shapes those development.

Alongside this empirical work, I am deeply interested in what developmental science can contribute to artificial intelligence. Children are the most remarkable learners we know. Understanding how they develop offers a blueprint for building AI systems that are safer, more adaptive, and more aligned with human values.

Research

Six areas of focus

Infant motor and language development

Tracking how infants acquire foundational motor skills and early language, and how development in one domain fuels progress in the other.

Core Empirical Work

Parent-child interaction

Examining how caregiver's cognitive factors and behaviors shape infant learning and developmental outcomes.

Environmental Factors

AI literacy and education

Understanding how children and caregivers think about AI, and how to build age-appropriate frameworks for learning and using intelligent systems.

Education and Impact

Developmental knowledge in AI and robotics

Applying what we know about how children learn to the design of more capable and human-aligned AI systems.

Translational Science

AI safety and ethics for children

Investigating the unique risks that AI systems pose to young users, and developing principled frameworks for child-centered AI design and governance.

Safety and Alignment

AI for psychological science

Exploring how large language models, computer vision, and machine learning methods can accelerate discovery in developmental and clinical psychology research.
